Primer on orientation

An orientation of a geometric space is a way of assigning a consistent "handedness" to the space. That's a little vague, so it demands some elaboration.

The concept of orientation arises from the observation that some objects are different from their mirror images, in the sense that after you mirror the object, there's no way to rotate or translate it so that the mirror image overlaps with the original. The classic example of this is the left hand as compared to the right, which is why orientation is often called handedness, or chirality ('handedness' in Greek-ish English).

It turns out that in ordinary Euclidean space, regardless of how many dimensions you're working in, there's only two distinct orientations. The formal fact corresponding to this intuitive fact is that the isometry group of Euclidean space has two connected components â€” if you restrict your attention to linear isometries, these will be the isometries with determinant 1 (which perserve orientation) and those with determinant -1 (which reverse orientation). There is a continuous path between any two isometries that preserve orientation, and between any two that reverse it, but there's no continuous way to go from an isometry that preserves orientation to one that reverses it, without passing through non-isometries.

As far as I know, that there are only two orientations is true in general spaces and not just Euclidean ones, but as the geometric interpretation of our space becomes more obscure, so does the geometric meaning of orientation.

On differentiable manifolds, an orientation can be thought of as a global section of the orientation bundle.

Manifold, bundle, section, orientation

I assume you know what vector spaces, bases, equivalence classes, \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and continuity are.

For a finite dimensional vector space, we say that two bases have equal orientation when the linear map sending one to the other has positive determinant. This splits the collection of all bases into two equivalence classes, and each of these equivalence classes is an orientation of V. (Alternatively, an orientation of V is a choice of which equivalence class is positive, which is not precisely the same thing.)

For an example, consider \(\mathbb{R}^2\) with basis vectors \(i,j\). If the shorter path for rotating \(i\) to align with \(j\) goes counterclockwise, then the basis is positively oriented (by convention). Otherwise the basis is negatively oriented.
